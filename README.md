# Urban Eco-Pulsing Water Infrastructure (UEPWI)

> Japanese version: [README_ja.md](./README_ja.md)

> This repository presents a conceptual proposal and theoretical framework. It does not claim that UEPWI is a proven system, nor that it can definitively solve urban heat islands, dust, or pollen problems. Scientific modeling, pilot testing, public health assessment, infrastructure review, and governance design are required before any real-world implementation.

## Overview

The **Urban Eco-Pulsing Water Infrastructure (UEPWI)** is a conceptual framework grounded in the philosophy of **Natural Complementary Science**. It explores the hypothesis that restoring localized hydrological cycles—essentially re-establishing a city's "metabolic" functions—could provide an integrated, multi-faceted approach to several interconnected urban environmental challenges.

The core idea is to treat the city not merely as a dry, heat-retaining artificial structure, but as an urban system that may regain partial "breathing" and "sweating" functions through controlled water circulation.

## CO₂ Reduction Position

UEPWI is **not** a replacement for CO₂ reduction. CO₂ reduction remains necessary. UEPWI is positioned as a complementary urban adaptation and mitigation-support concept. While reducing global emissions remains a central long-term goal, cities also require immediate, localized adaptation strategies to manage acute environmental stressors.

## 1. Background

Modern urban environments are characterized by extensive impervious surfaces such as asphalt and concrete, together with rapid stormwater drainage systems. This infrastructure design has largely eliminated natural processes of water infiltration and evaporation, leading to a disrupted urban water cycle.

As a result, cities face compounded environmental stressors:

- **Urban Heat Island (UHI) Effect**: Heat-absorbing surfaces increase ambient and surface temperatures.
- **Dry Surfaces and Dust**: The lack of surface moisture allows particulate matter such as PM2.5 and PM10 to resuspend into the air due to wind and traffic.
- **Airborne Allergens**: Pollen grains may travel further and remain airborne longer in dry, hot urban air.
- **Stormwater Loss**: Large volumes of rainwater are rapidly channeled away, representing a lost resource while increasing downstream flood risks.

## 2. Original Concept

The core idea of UEPWI is to transform the city from a dry, heat-retaining structure into an environment that can "sweat" and "breathe" through controlled water circulation.

This includes:

- **Underground Rainwater Tanks**: Capturing and storing stormwater runoff in large subterranean cisterns.
- **Automated Irrigation and Misting Systems**: Deploying targeted misting and irrigation systems across roads, sidewalks, medians, street trees, and green corridors.
- **Solar-Powered Pumps**: Using renewable energy such as solar panels to power water distribution, aiming for lower-carbon operation and reduced operational emissions.
- **Water Filtration**: Implementing rigorous filtration and sterilization systems to ensure the safety of aerosolized or publicly distributed water.
- **Integrated Water Sourcing**: Balancing harvested rainwater, safely treated household greywater, and carefully managed river water intake where ecologically appropriate.

## 3. Proposed Mechanisms

The proposed system hypothesizes the following mechanisms of action:

- **Evaporative Cooling**: The phase change of water from liquid to vapor absorbs latent heat, potentially cooling the surrounding air.
- **Surface Temperature Reduction**: Applying water to heated asphalt and concrete may reduce surface temperature and subsequent heat radiation.
- **Dust Resuspension Suppression**: Dampening surfaces increases cohesive forces between dust particles and the ground, reducing resuspension into the air.
- **Pollen Capture**: Aerosolized water droplets may physically intercept airborne pollen grains and wash them to the ground.
- **Vegetation Transpiration Support**: Consistent watering of urban green infrastructure may improve plant health, enhancing shading and biological transpiration.
- **Reduction of Cooling Energy Demand**: Lowering ambient urban temperatures could theoretically reduce Cooling Degree Days (CDD) and the energy required for air conditioning.
- **Flood Mitigation**: Capturing stormwater at the source may reduce peak load on municipal drainage systems.

## 4. Seasonal Considerations

UEPWI is conceived as a dynamic system that adapts to seasonal environmental stressors:

- **Summer**: Primary focus on UHI mitigation through high-frequency misting and surface cooling.
- **Spring**: Focus on pollen suppression through targeted misting during peak pollen dispersal periods.
- **Dry Seasons**: Focus on dust control and maintaining limited surface moisture where appropriate.
- **Winter / Freezing Risk**: The system requires strict temperature-based shutoff and automated purge protocols to prevent pipe bursts and hazardous road icing.
- **Dry-Air / Influenza Hypothesis**: There is a theoretical hypothesis that increasing localized winter humidity might reduce the viability of certain airborne viruses, such as influenza. This is strictly a hypothesis and not a proven medical claim.

## 5. System Architecture

A full UEPWI deployment would theoretically require:

- **Collection**: Catchment areas, storm drains, and greywater diversion systems.
- **Storage**: Subterranean cisterns equipped with overflow management.
- **Filtration**: Multi-stage filtration and sterilization, such as mechanical filtration, UV treatment, and possible chlorination, to prevent pathogen transmission.
- **Distribution**: A network of low-flow pipes, misting nozzles, and drip irrigation lines.
- **Sensors**: Environmental monitoring networks for temperature, humidity, soil moisture, PM levels, and potentially pollen counts.
- **Control Algorithms**: AI-driven or rule-based systems to optimize water release based on real-time sensor data and weather forecasts.
- **Renewable Energy**: Integrated photovoltaic arrays and battery storage where feasible.
- **Maintenance**: Regular filter replacement, sensor calibration, nozzle cleaning, pipe flushing, and seasonal shutdown procedures.

## 6. Safety and Limitations Summary

Implementing UEPWI poses significant challenges. Key limitations include the risk of waterborne pathogens such as Legionella if filtration fails, the possibility of worsening thermal comfort by over-humidifying already hot and humid environments, high capital and maintenance costs, complex water rights governance, and the engineering difficulty of retrofitting dense urban areas.
